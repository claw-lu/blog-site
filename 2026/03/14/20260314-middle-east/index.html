The achievement represents a significant shift in the Saudi economy’s structure and demonstrates the viability of the ambitious reform agenda.

The discovery, made by a joint Egyptian-European archaeological team, includes a small pyramid, associated burial chambers, and numerous artifacts that provide new insights into ancient Egyptian burial practices. Dr. Mostafa Waziri, Secretary-General of the Supreme Council of Antiquities, described the find as one of the most significant discoveries in recent years. The complex appears to have belonged to a previously unknown pharaoh or high-ranking official from the Fifth Dynasty, around 2500 BCE. Archaeologists have uncovered hieroglyphic inscriptions, painted wall decorations, and well-preserved burial goods including jewelry, pottery, and religious artifacts. The discovery is expected to attract significant international attention and boost Egypt’s tourism industry, which has been recovering steadily following recent global challenges. Advanced conservation techniques are being employed to preserve the findings while making them accessible for future research and public viewing. International museums have already expressed interest in collaborative exhibitions featuring artifacts from the discovery. The Egyptian government plans to incorporate the site into existing tourist circuits connecting major archaeological destinations around Cairo.
